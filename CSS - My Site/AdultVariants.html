Mu

  • Also known as "the cute one"; my classmates and I remember Mu's location by imagining cat ears on top of the head, in the centroparietal area
  • 7-11 Hz
  • Most common in teens and young adults
  • Twice as common in females
  • Notched appearance
  • The sharp element is negative (and therefore, deflects upwards)
  • Found in the awake state
  • Attenuates with movement or thinking about moving

Wicket Spikes

  • 6-11 Hz
  • Notched appearance
  • Temporal regions; my classmates and I remember this by imagining "wicked" devil horns on the sides of the head
  • Appears in wake, drowsy, and light sleep
  • More common in adults 30+ years
  • Amplitude ranges from 60-100 μV

RMTTD

  • It's all in the name: Rhythmic Mid-Temporal Theta of Drowsiness
  • 4-7 Hz
  • May have double time fast waves
  • Appears in drowsiness and light sleep
  • Maximal in temporal regions
  • Amplitude is medium to high (20+ μV)

Lambda

  • Waves look like a sailboat
  • Low voltage
  • Posterior, mostly occipital
  • Appears when awake, eyes scanning horizontally
  • Suppresses posterior dominant rhythm

SREDA

  • Subclinical Rhythmic EDEG Discharges of Adults
  • Pattern consists of monophasic sharp waves that repeat every 1-2 seconds, gradulaly eveolving to 4-7 Hz pattern
  • Usually symmetric and synchronous
  • Rare
  • When it does show up, primarily in adults 50+
  • Temporal-parietal distribution
  • 2 types of onset: abrupt and gradual
  • Duration is ~20+ seconds

6 Per Second Spike & Wave

  • 4-7 Hz
  • Duration less than 2 seconds
  • Appears in wakefulness to mild drowsy
  • 2 components: WHAM and FOLD
  • WHAM: Waking record, High amplitude, Anterior in location, Males
  • FOLD: Females, Occipital emphasis, Low amplitude, Drowsy

SSS/BETS

  • SSS: Small Sharp Spikes
  • BETS: Benign Eliptiform Transients of Sleep
  • Quick, low voltage spikes
  • Positive and negative waves will have similar amplitude
  • Unilateral or bilateral
  • Temporal lobes, sometimes posterior frontal regions
